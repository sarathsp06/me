<SectionHeading icon="fa-lightbulb" color="amber">The Idea</SectionHeading>

<div class="prose-content">

In SRE, an [error budget](https://sre.google/workbook/error-budget-policy/) defines how much unreliability you can tolerate before pausing feature development to focus on stability. If your service targets 99.9% uptime, you have a budget of 0.1% downtime per month. Use it up and the team stops shipping features until reliability is restored.

I have been wondering -- what if we applied this concept to Agile sprints?

Rather than tracking downtime in minutes, we would measure **developer effort spent fixing bugs, handling incidents, and improving reliability**. Every point used here is one not invested in building features.

What if this became our sprint's "error budget"?

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-2 gap-4 my-6">
  <div style="background: linear-gradient(135deg, #f0fdf4, #f7fef9); border: 1px solid #bbf7d0; border-radius: 10px; padding: 1.5rem;">
    <div class="flex items-center gap-3 mb-3">
      <span class="w-7 h-7 rounded-full bg-emerald-600 text-white flex items-center justify-center text-xs font-bold"><i class="fas fa-check"></i></span>
      <h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-gray-900">Stay within budget</h3>
    </div>
    <div class="prose-content">
      <p class="mb-0">Continue delivering features. The team is in a healthy state -- bugs and reliability work are within expected norms. No action needed.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div style="background: linear-gradient(135deg, #fef2f2, #fff5f5); border: 1px solid #fecaca; border-radius: 10px; padding: 1.5rem;">
    <div class="flex items-center gap-3 mb-3">
      <span class="w-7 h-7 rounded-full bg-red-600 text-white flex items-center justify-center text-xs font-bold"><i class="fas fa-exclamation"></i></span>
      <h3 class="text-lg font-semibold text-gray-900">Exceed budget</h3>
    </div>
    <div class="prose-content">
      <p class="mb-0">Shift to stability work until balance is restored. The team is accumulating too much unplanned work -- adding features on top of a shaky foundation will only make things worse.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="prose-content">

As the product becomes more stable over time, the budget percentage decreases, creating more sprint capacity for new work. This is the virtuous cycle: investing in stability today pays dividends in velocity tomorrow.

</div>

<SectionHeading icon="fa-calculator" color="blue">How It Works</SectionHeading>

<div class="prose-content">

The concept maps cleanly from SRE to sprint planning. Instead of an SLO (Service Level Objective) measured in uptime, you set a stability allocation measured in story points or effort hours.

</div>

</Callout>

<SectionHeading icon="fa-list-ol" color="emerald">Implementation Approach</SectionHeading>

<div class="prose-content mb-6">

Here is how I would roll this out with a team. The approach has five steps, each building on the previous one.

</div>

<StepList>
  <StepItem number={1} title="Set a Bug Budget">
    <div class="prose-content">
      <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Begin with total sprint capacity (e.g., 150 points). Allocate a percentage for bugs and reliability work based on your product's current stability:</p>
    </div>
    <div class="grid grid-cols-1 sm:grid-cols-3 gap-3 mb-3">
      <div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-3 text-center">
        <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-gray-800 mb-1">20%</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-gray-500">30 points</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-emerald-600 font-medium">Stable product</p>
      </div>
      <div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-3 text-center">
        <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-gray-800 mb-1">25%</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-gray-500">37.5 points</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-amber-600 font-medium">Growing product</p>
      </div>
      <div class="bg-white border border-gray-200 rounded-lg p-3 text-center">
        <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-gray-800 mb-1">30%</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-gray-500">45 points</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-red-600 font-medium">High defect rate</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 leading-relaxed">If you are unsure where to start, look at the last three sprints. What percentage of completed points were bug fixes, incidents, or reliability work? That is your baseline. Set the budget slightly below it -- the goal is to improve, not to rubber-stamp the status quo.</p>
  </StepItem>

  <StepItem number={2} title="Track Separately">
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">Distinguish bug and reliability tickets from feature tickets in your sprint board. Tag them, use a separate swimlane, or filter by ticket type -- whatever fits your tooling. The point is visibility: at any moment during the sprint, you should be able to see how much of the error budget has been consumed.</p>
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 leading-relaxed">Monitor how quickly the budget depletes during the sprint. A budget that is 80% consumed by day 3 of a 10-day sprint is a leading indicator that something is wrong, even if the sprint as a whole is on track.</p>
  </StepItem>

  <StepItem number={3} title="Establish Triggers">
    <div class="prose-content">
      <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 leading-relaxed mb-3">This is where the error budget concept earns its keep. Define clear, agreed-upon triggers:</p>
    </div>
    <div class="grid grid-cols-1 sm:grid-cols-2 gap-3 mb-3">
      <div class="bg-white border border-amber-200 rounded-lg p-3">
        <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-gray-800 mb-1"><i class="fas fa-exclamation-triangle text-amber-500 mr-1"></i> Budget exhausted mid-sprint</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-gray-500">Reduce or pause feature work to address quality issues. The team shifts focus until stability is restored.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="bg-white border border-red-200 rounded-lg p-3">
        <p class="font-semibold text-sm text-gray-800 mb-1"><i class="fas fa-ban text-red-500 mr-1"></i> Repeated overruns (2+ sprints)</p>
        <p class="text-xs text-gray-500">Schedule dedicated reliability work in upcoming sprints. This is no longer a one-off -- there is a systemic issue that needs investment.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </StepItem>

  <StepItem number={4} title="Adjust Over Time">
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 leading-relaxed">As stability improves, lower the bug budget percentage. This is the reward for investing in quality: every percentage point you can reduce the error budget is a percentage point that becomes available for feature work. Stability pays for itself -- better reliability creates more capacity for new features.</p>
  </StepItem>

  <StepItem number={5} title="Review at Sprint End">
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600 leading-relaxed">Compare actual bug effort against the budget during the retrospective. Use historical data to refine future allocations. Over several sprints, you will build a reliable picture of your team's true stability cost -- and whether it is trending in the right direction.</p>
  </StepItem>
</StepList>

<SectionHeading icon="fa-check-circle" color="emerald">Why This Could Work</SectionHeading>

<div class="prose-content mb-6">

The sprint error budget is not a radical idea. It is a reframing of something most teams already do informally -- allocating some capacity for bugs. The difference is making it explicit, measurable, and consequential.

</div>

<SectionHeading icon="fa-triangle-exclamation" color="red">Where It Could Go Wrong</SectionHeading>

<div class="prose-content mb-6">

This is still experimental, and I can see several ways it might fail or be misapplied.

</div>

<div class="space-y-3">
  <PitfallCard title="Gaming the classification">
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600">If exceeding the bug budget has consequences, teams may be tempted to reclassify bugs as "enhancements" or "tech debt" to stay under budget. The fix is cultural, not mechanical: the budget is a diagnostic tool, not a punishment. If the team is consistently reclassifying work to avoid the trigger, the problem is the trigger's consequences, not the classification.</p>
  </PitfallCard>

  <PitfallCard title="Setting the budget too low too early">
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600">A team that sets a 10% error budget on a product with a 30% defect rate will blow through it every sprint and quickly learn to ignore it. Start with a budget slightly below your current baseline and reduce it gradually. The goal is incremental improvement, not aspirational targets that nobody hits.</p>
  </PitfallCard>

  <PitfallCard title="Punishing teams for exceeding the budget">
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600">The error budget is a signal, not a scorecard. If leadership uses budget overruns to criticize the team, engineers will stop reporting bugs honestly. The correct response to an exceeded budget is "what systemic issue is causing this, and how do we invest in fixing it?" -- not "why can't you stay within budget?"</p>
  </PitfallCard>

  <PitfallCard title="Ignoring the difference between planned and unplanned bug work">
    <p class="text-sm text-gray-600">Proactive reliability work (improving test coverage, refactoring fragile code) and reactive bug fixes (production incidents, customer-reported defects) are both "bug budget" items, but they mean very different things about your team's health. A team spending 25% on proactive reliability is in a different place than a team spending 25% on firefighting. Track both, but interpret them differently.</p>
  </PitfallCard>
</div>

<SectionHeading icon="fa-flag-checkered" color="gray">Closing Thoughts</SectionHeading>

<div class="prose-content">

This remains experimental. I have not implemented it across enough sprints to consider it proven. The idea is straightforward -- borrow a concept that works well in SRE and adapt it for sprint planning -- but the details of implementation matter enormously. The right budget percentage, the right triggers, the right cultural framing -- all of these need to be tuned to the specific team and product.

I am curious: would this approach work with your team, or would it just complicate sprint planning? The answer probably depends on whether your team already has a clear understanding of how much effort goes to bugs versus features. If you do not know that number, finding it out might be the most valuable first step -- regardless of whether you adopt the error budget framework.

</div>

<Callout type="tip" title="Try This Monday" icon="fa-rocket">
  <p class="text-sm text-gray-600">Before adopting any of this, run one experiment: look at your last three sprints and calculate what percentage of completed points were bug fixes, incidents, or reliability work. If the number surprises you -- and it usually does -- you have found the starting point for your error budget.</p>
</Callout>
